Priority 2 — Week 1

Your Daily Intelligence Brief

This is your morning command center. Every day you get a crisp brief — what came in overnight, what's on your calendar, what you need to move today. No inbox diving, no Slack spelunking. You start in control instead of reactive.

Start by running this manually each morning. Once it's delivering value, automate it as a 6:30am cron push straight to your Telegram.

💡 To automate: In OpenClaw, type /cron and create a daily job at 6:30am running the prompt below. It'll push your brief to Telegram before you're out of bed.

Daily Intelligence Brief Copy & Paste into OpenClaw
Run my daily intelligence brief. Here's what I need: **1. OVERNIGHT SUMMARY** Scan my email and Slack from the last 18 hours. Pull out anything that: - Requires a decision or response from me today - Is time-sensitive (someone waiting on me, a deadline approaching) - Is unusual, unexpected, or escalated from normal Ignore: newsletters, marketing, automated notifications, receipts. Flag anything from my direct reports, key vendors, or clients as higher priority. **2. TODAY'S SCHEDULE** Pull my calendar for today. For each meeting, tell me: - What it is and who's in it - Any prep I should do (based on context you know about this person/topic) - Flag any conflicts or back-to-backs I should know about **3. TOP 3 PRIORITIES FOR TODAY** Based on my email, calendar, and anything I told you recently — give me the 3 things I absolutely must move forward today. Not a task list — the 3 things that, if I do nothing else, make today a win. **4. ANYTHING I SHOULD WATCH FOR** Flag anything that looks like it could blow up, needs a fast decision, or that I'm at risk of dropping. One or two sentences per item. No noise — only real flags. Format this cleanly. Keep it tight. I want to read it in under 3 minutes.
✓ Run this manually for one week first. Once it's consistently useful, automate the 6:30am push. That's when it becomes a real system — it shows up before you go looking for it.
💡 Phase 2 — Hourly Email Heartbeat: After the morning brief is dialed in, add a second cron that fires every hour during business hours. A quick push to Telegram: "here's what came in since your last check, here's what needs your attention." No inbox diving. Just signal. This is the proactive filtering you described — staying aware of your email without living in it. Set it up Week 2.
Priority 3 — Week 2

Email Triage — For You and Michelle

You were clear: you don't want AI sending emails autonomously. You want the signal filtered and surfaced so you and Michelle can act on what matters. That's exactly what this does. It reads your inbox, categorizes everything, archives the noise, and hands you a clean action list with draft responses ready for your review. You steer, it drafts, you send.

Email Triage Prompt Copy & Paste into OpenClaw
Run a full email triage on my inbox. Here's how I want you to work: **STEP 1 — CATEGORIZE** Go through the last 48 hours of email (or all unread, whichever is more). Sort every email into one of these buckets: - 🔴 ACTION REQUIRED — needs my response or a decision, today - 🟡 DELEGATE TO MICHELLE — she can handle or respond to this - 🟢 FYI / ARCHIVE — no action needed, safe to archive - ⚫ NOISE — newsletters, automated, marketing — archive immediately **STEP 2 — SURFACE THE REDS** For each 🔴 item: - Who it's from and what they need - What I should say back (draft a response — 3-5 sentences, direct, written in my voice) - How urgent it actually is (respond today, or can it wait 24h?) **STEP 3 — BRIEF MICHELLE** For each 🟡 item, write a one-line instruction Michelle can act on: Example: "Reply to [name] — confirm the Tuesday appointment, ask them to send the contract first." **STEP 4 — ARCHIVE REPORT** Tell me what you archived and why, so I can spot-check your judgment. One line each. **RULES:** - Never draft a response that commits me to something without flagging it - Anything from a client, vendor, or lawyer = high priority regardless of category - If something feels off — unusual request, angry tone, legal/financial risk — put it at the top and tell me why - Keep drafts in my voice: direct, no fluff, get to the point fast
💡 For Michelle: Have her run this prompt at 8am and 2pm each day. She'll have a prioritized action list before she opens a single email manually. Within a week she'll be processing 2x the volume in half the time.
⚠ Exchange Server heads-up: You're on Microsoft 365 with Exchange Server on-premise — not Gmail. OpenClaw's standard email integration is OAuth-based (Google/standard providers). For your setup, you'll need to enable IMAP or Microsoft Graph API access first. This is a one-time configuration step — flag it with your IT contact before running triage, or bring it to your follow-up call with Caleb and he'll walk through it.

⚠ Start read-only for the first two weeks. OpenClaw drafts and flags — you or Michelle reviews and sends. Once you're approving ~80% of drafts without changes, you can expand Michelle's authority to approve directly. Don't skip this trust-building phase.

Traps to Avoid

Things That Will Slow You Down

  • Full email autonomy too early: You were right to pump the brakes here. Start with triage and flagging. Build trust over 60 days before expanding what it can do autonomously.
  • Migrating your comm stack before your AI stack is stable: You're weighing Slack vs. Teams. Don't migrate until your AI workflows are running smoothly. Adding infrastructure disruption on top of workflow change is a recipe for nothing getting adopted.
  • Letting the team stay at ChatGPT level: Awareness without leverage is waste. Michelle needs one specific workflow to own — not general AI access. Start there. Her success creates the pull for the rest of the team.
  • Over-running the adversarial agent setup: It's a great tool — use it for high-stakes decisions (M&A, legal, hiring strategy). Not daily ops. The compute cost and review burden add up fast.
  • Automating everything at once: Pick one workflow, run it for two weeks, get it to 90% accurate, then add the next. The discipline of going one at a time is what makes the whole system stick.
